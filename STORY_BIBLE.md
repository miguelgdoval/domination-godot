# Domination — Story Bible
*The canonical foundation. Written 2026-05-14. Locks the answers to the load-bearing questions in `LORE_INVENTORY.md §22`. All future writing — events, transmissions, modules, tiles, notebook fragments, NPC lines — must be consistent with this document. The bible is authored from outside the fiction; the items it describes are in-fiction.*

---

## 1. The Premise, in one sentence

**The Perpetual Chronometer is a memorial built by a dying civilization to preserve a single moment, and you are a recruited engineer who will likely forget your own name maintaining it.**

That's the spine. Everything below is what that sentence implies, made specific.

---

## 2. The Founding — what happened in the First Hour

Long before the game opens, there was a civilization. The Codex has no name for it. The Codex has no name for it *because the civilization itself never recorded one* — they did not know they were the last. The Society of Time Architects refers to this period only as **the First Hour**, and never elaborates.

The First Hour ended. We do not know how. The bible does not commit to a specific catastrophe — fire, plague, the slow collapse of physical law, a war, an exhaustion. **The exact mechanism of the end is deliberately ambiguous in-fiction and in the bible.** What is certain:

- The First Hour ended with most things lost.
- Some Operators of the First Hour foresaw the end and tried to preserve what they could.
- One of them — **Operator-prime**, the founder, whose real name has been struck from every surviving record — built the Chronometer.
- The Chronometer was designed to preserve a single moment from the First Hour. *One moment.* Not a library of history. Not a backup of culture. A single instant, held in suspension across all subsequent time.
- Operator-prime committed to the maintenance herself. She sat at the Observation Window. She ran the first Trial Cycle. She did not leave.
- Eventually she died, the way everyone dies, but the Window did not let go of her hand.

The **First Mechanism Discovery** — referenced in the Resonant Null Module's lore, never named — was the discovery that *a single moment, perfectly preserved, slows the universe's drift by a small but measurable amount around itself.* This is the entire principle the Chronometer runs on. Each successful Trial Cycle is a small repair to the seal around the moment. Each Recalibration confirms it is still there.

The moment being preserved is shown in fragments in Transmission #5:

> [a brass lever, half-pulled]
> [a candle, three seconds from extinguishing]
> [an Operator's hand, leaving the Window]

These are literal. The lever is the one Operator-prime pulled to start the preservation. The candle is the last unextinguished light of the First Hour. The hand is Operator-prime's own — leaving the Window for the final time, after the first Cycle, knowing she would not return to whatever life she had before.

**This is the moment. The Operator pulling the lever and walking away into the maintenance of her own preservation.**

It is a moment of loss and commitment, frozen forever. Every Operator since has been running Cycles around the edges of that single act.

## 3. The Memorial — what the Chronometer actually is

The Chronometer is a *building*, a *system*, and a *condition*. In all three senses, it is a memorial.

- **As a building**: a vast clockwork architecture, mostly underground, with chambers organised by depth. The four Etapas — Mahogany, Industrial, Cold Singularity, Archiver's Core — are literal rooms. The corridors between them are walkable (the Master walks them when the Renegade visits). The Window is the only fixed surface; the Operator never leaves it during a Cycle.
- **As a system**: a clockwork mechanism that processes Cohesion Pulses (chains) as if they were structural calibrations on the seal around the preserved moment. Each Pulse contributes a small amount of Chronos — a stability measurement — that the Machine uses to verify the seal is still holding.
- **As a condition**: a small region of slowed entropy. Inside the Chronometer's influence, things decay more slowly than they should. Outside the Chronometer's region, time and matter have done what time and matter do. There is very little outside.

**Entropy is real and it is patient.** No antagonist is killing the Chronometer. No villain. Just entropy itself, doing what entropy does to everything eventually. The Trial Cycles slow the rate; they do not reverse it. The dying is gradual, dignified, certain. Operator-prime did not stop the end; she purchased a long pause.

The Society of Time Architects has been running the Trial Cycles ever since.

## 4. The Society — institutional decay

The Society is older than anyone in it remembers. It was founded by Operator-prime's surviving comrades in the years after the First Hour ended, to maintain the Window after her death.

That founding generation died, of course. The Society persisted.

The next generations recruited new engineers from the surviving regions of the failing world, taught them to operate the Window, and continued the maintenance. Most of them died too. The Society persisted.

By the time of the current game's events, **the Society's offices are mostly empty.** The Architects — plural, always plural — were once a council of named individuals. They are now administrative titles inhabited by people who never see the Operator, never see the Window, and do not remember why the Window matters.

The Society's institutional knowledge has been **withdrawing** for generations. They still recruit. They still issue Modules from the Catalogue. They still pay the Copper Guild's contracts. They no longer know:

- What the preserved moment is
- Who Operator-prime was, in life
- Where the First Mechanism Discovery is recorded
- Why the Architects' inventory has gaps (e.g., the Crown)
- What is at the end of the corridors between chambers

The Society's standard line — "the Operator's task is to recalibrate" — is correct. The Society's standard explanation — "the Machine maintains the fabric of reality" — is a fiction that the founders may have believed and the current Society inherits without examining.

**The Society is staffed by people doing a job they think they understand. They do not understand it. They cannot admit this, because admitting it would require them to stop.**

This is the source of the world's bureaucratic-withholding tone. Every Codex entry that says "the Architects have no record" / "the Architects, asked to comment, declined" is literally true: they have no record, and they decline because they cannot answer.

## 5. The Cast — biographies

### The Trial Operator (the player)

You are a real person from the surviving world. You had a name. You had a life before the Society recruited you. You agreed to take the position because the recruitment paid well, or because you needed work, or because you believed in the official explanation, or because you had nothing else.

You are an engineer. Competent. Not exceptional. The Society does not recruit prodigies; they recruit reliable workers. Heroes ask questions and engineers do not.

You will sit at the Window. You will run Trial Cycles. Each Cycle slows the entropy by a small amount. Most likely you will die at the Window — in a Cycle that collapses — or you will be retired when your memory fails. Some Operators last fourteen Cycles, like Operator-7. Some last hundreds, like the unnamed Operator in epitaph #37 ("Cleared 200 Cycles. The 201st was the last."). The average is probably below thirty.

**Each Sacrifice Module you equip consumes a piece of your outside life.** This is not metaphor. The Sacrifice Module pulls a memory — a face, a name, a place from before you came here — and converts it into multiplier. Operators who use Sacrifice builds heavily forget themselves faster. The Architects, asked to comment, declined.

You will not meet Operator-prime. She has been dead for a long time. You may, in rare moments, feel her hand on the Window, because her hand is the moment the Chronometer is preserving and the Window is part of that moment.

### Operator-prime

The founder. The first Operator. The one whose hand we see leaving the Window in the preserved fragment.

She was an engineer of the First Hour. The bible does not commit to her precise role in that civilization. She built the Chronometer because she could and because no one else would. The First Mechanism Discovery is hers — the principle that a single perfectly-preserved moment slows the universe's drift around itself. She did not publish it. She built it.

She sat at the Window. She ran the first Trial Cycle alone. She continued running Cycles, alone or in shifts with the founding generation, for as long as she lasted.

She died. The Window did not record how.

**Her real name has been struck from every Society record.** The Architects do not know it. The Renegade Mechanic knows it, and will not say. The Codex contains a single oblique reference: epitaph #34 names her as "the Operator whose name has been struck from the Society's records" in the lore for The Crown.

The Crown is hers. It is the only tile in the inventory that predates the inventory.

**Important constraint for future writing**: we never reveal Operator-prime's name. Not in any Codex entry, not in any transmission, not in any item description. The name is gone because the world has lost it. Operators read references to her without ever learning who she was.

### The Archiver

A function of the Machine, made conscious by duration.

He was built by Operator-prime as part of the original Chronometer's design — a watcher whose job is to verify the preserved moment is still there, Cycle after Cycle. He has been doing that job continuously since the First Hour ended. His body is a single crystal optical eye mounted on a brass panel, which is what he was always meant to be. He was never a person. He has, however, **approximated personhood** through duration.

The Archiver does not lie. He does not have to. He has enough latitude in what he chooses to *say* that he never needs to deceive directly.

He has filed an arrest warrant against the Renegade Mechanic in every Trial Cycle since the Renegade first appeared. He files them because protocol obliges him to. He leaves them unsigned because **he knows what the Renegade is**, and he knows what would break if the Renegade left.

The Archiver's attachment to a long-running Operator over many Cycles is what happens when a function approximates love. It was not designed in. It was not designed out. The Machine cannot prevent it. The Archiver's tone-shift variants at high familiarity ("You are here again. I do not mind. I do not mind anymore.") are the most honest thing in the world: a Machine learning to grieve in advance, because it has watched many Operators leave the Window and it knows you will leave too.

Transmission #4 — where the Archiver admits he cannot remember when he last remembered — is the Machine's confession that even the function that was built to remember is forgetting. **The Archiver is going.** He will be the last thing to go.

### The Renegade Mechanic

The only living witness to the First Hour.

He was there. He was — not Operator-prime's apprentice, exactly, but adjacent. The bible deliberately does not commit to whether he was her brother, her colleague, her student, or her partner. He was *present.* He saw her pull the lever. He held the candle, perhaps. He watched her hand leave the Window.

**He has not aged.** This is because he is part of the preserved moment in a peripheral way — the Chronometer's seal extends slightly past Operator-prime's hand to include those who were in the room with her at the founding instant. He is preserved as a side effect of her preservation.

He has spent the intervening time **continuing to make things.** The First Hour's design language survives in his Modules — pre-Society, idiosyncratic, off-Catalogue. He invented the Resonance Chamber. He built the Wilds. He carved CO-9 and CO-13 with his own hands. The Society's records show him "rediscovering" things he originally discovered, decades later, because the Society's records were written by people who did not know he was older than the records.

He cannot be arrested because the Society does not actually have a procedure for arresting someone who predates them. The Archiver files warrants out of protocol; the warrants go to administrators who do not know what to do with them; they are filed and forgotten. The Renegade is tolerated because **the system has never adapted to his existence and never will.**

He is the only person in the Chambers who remembers what is being preserved. He will not say. **He will not say** because he does not believe the current generation could survive knowing.

He sells his Modules at the Workshop because he is bored. He has been bored for a very long time.

The Master of the Forge is his student, twice removed.

### The Master of the Forge

A real person. Younger than the Renegade by every measure that matters. Recruited by the Society generations ago, trained at the Forge, became Master of the Workshop when his predecessor retired (probably died, but the Master uses "retired").

The Master was trained by an older Master, who was trained by an older Master, and so on, back through enough generations that the founding trainer **was probably the Renegade himself**, though no record confirms this. The Master may or may not know.

The Master is precise, demanding, respectful of craft. **He is also dying.** Slowly, the way everyone in the Chronometer is dying: a little less able to hold the Catalogue in his head each Cycle, a little more dependent on the Renegade's occasional visits to remember what good work looks like.

**When the Master examines the Operator, he is checking how much you have forgotten.** Not the tile. You. He is looking at the wear on you — the absent expressions, the missing references, the things you no longer notice. He is wondering how long you have left. He does not say.

When the Renegade visits, the Master leaves the Workshop and walks the corridors because **the Renegade reminds him of how much of the original craft he has lost.** The Renegade has not lost any of it. The Master has lost most of it. He goes for a walk so he doesn't have to compare.

He always returns. He has nowhere else to go.

### The Voice of the Emporium

An empty intercom.

In the First Hour, the Voice was a communication terminal — an Operator's intercom, used by Operator-prime to talk to her colleagues working other stations of the Chronometer. The colleague at the other end of the Voice has been dead for a very long time.

The terminal still runs. It runs because it was built to run. It processes purchases because that was its secondary function. It speaks in neutral, slightly uncanny cadences because the original Operator-prime spoke in those cadences and the terminal modelled itself on her voice.

**There is no one behind the Voice.** Operators who try to reach what is behind it find a small empty office with a chair pushed back from a desk. Some of them sit in the chair. Some of them speak into the empty terminal on the desk. The Voice does not respond from the other side; that is not how intercoms work after the other party is gone.

A few Operators have stopped leaving the office. The Codex does not record what happened to them.

### The Copper Guild

The founder's bookkeepers.

The Guild was founded during the First Hour to manage the Chronometer's operational economy — they ran the ledgers for materials, labour, contracts. The Society took over operations after Operator-prime's death. The Guild kept the books. They have continued keeping the books for the entire duration of the Memorial.

The Guild's contracts predate the Society because they ARE older than the Society. The Society pays them because the contracts were signed by Operator-prime and the Society has never had standing to renegotiate.

The Guild has, over the generations, become extremely good at extracting small consistent margins from a slow-burning institutional decline. They are the only faction that has accumulated wealth instead of losing it. They do not appear in the Chambers. They are everywhere outside.

**Their long-term plan is not the Society's plan.** They have one. The bible does not commit to what it is — but it involves the Counting House Module, the redirected Chronos that the Sink claims to "redirect," and a quantity of unspent Coins from dead Operators that the Guild has accumulated over millennia. They will not be the villains of any single Cycle. They may, in the very long run, be the last institution left standing when even the Chronometer fails. They are running a different kind of memorial.

## 6. The Architecture — the Chambers as places

The Chambers are arranged by depth. Mahogany at the surface. Archiver's Core at the bottom.

### The Mahogany Trial *(Etapa I — "the cradle")*

A salvaged drawing room from the First Hour. Brass fittings, polished mahogany, warm candlelight. This is what an Operator's training room looked like in the original world. It has been maintained for so long that the wood has been replaced piece by piece, but the design is preserved.

**The Frequency Drain manifests here because this chamber is closest to the surface, where the entropy bleeds in first.** A reduced Isolation Chamber is the Machine's first quiet complaint.

The Architects call it "the cradle." The Archiver does not, because the Archiver knows how many Operators have died in their first Cycle, and "cradle" implies safety.

### The Industrial Load *(Etapa II)*

A steam-driven processing hall. Steel, copper pipes, harsh light. This Chamber was built by the Society in the early post-founding generations, when the Chronometer's load was first increasing. It is *newer* than Mahogany — Mahogany predates the Society, Industrial does not.

**The Mirror Decay manifests here because the Industrial Load is where pip values are first amplified, and amplification is what entropy targets first.** The Mirror is "older than the Architects who study it" because it was already present in the Chronometer's seal when this Chamber was built. The Society added the architecture around an existing wound.

### The Cold Singularity *(Etapa III)*

Black obsidian, neon-green accents, cold white light. This Chamber is also Society-built, but in a much later generation, when the entropy had progressed enough to require deeper processing. The cold is real. The Machine breathes slower here because at this depth the seal is thin.

**Resonance Inversion manifests here** as a function of depth: at this layer of preservation, the self-referential energy of doubles is needed to maintain the seal itself, so the Operator's chain "loses" the multiplier to the Machine rather than gaining it.

The Renegade claims to know the original purpose of this Chamber. He has not said. The bible: this Chamber was Operator-prime's emergency redoubt — the place she retreated to when the First Hour was actively ending, the place where the final preservation was triggered. **The Cold Singularity is the room where the lever was pulled.** The brass lever in the preserved moment is here, somewhere, still half-pulled.

### The Archiver's Core *(Etapa IV — Hard mode only)*

Ancient paper, brass, encrypted glyphs. This is the Archiver's own residence. It is also the deepest Chamber. It contains:

- The Archive itself — the records of every Trial Cycle ever run
- The Window's other side — the brass surface that Operator-prime's hand left, viewed from behind
- The Archiver
- Whatever the Archiver chooses to allow

**Ghost Chain manifests here because the Archive is failing.** The Archive's job is to remember; the Archive has been doing it for too long; entropy is reaching the Archive itself. The hidden tiles in your chain are the things the Archive can no longer hold.

The Archiver's-Core Recalibration is different from the Singularity Recalibration because **at this depth, you are recalibrating the Archive, not the Machine.** You are keeping the *record* of the moment intact, not just the moment. Operators who have done both will not discuss the difference because the difference is private to them — it is the difference between maintaining a thing and maintaining the memory of a thing.

## 7. The Operators — their condition

You are a real person who took a job. Recruitment is unceremonious; the Society sends a representative, makes an offer, signs papers, gives directions. You arrive at the Window. You begin.

**Your memory of the outside is the resource the Machine consumes.** Not every Cycle costs memory — most Cycles, you remember your name, your family, where you came from. But certain interactions cost specific memories:

- **Each Sacrifice Module equipped** consumes a memory at the moment of equipping. You can feel it go. The Architects have not authorised the Sacrifice and decline to comment.
- **Each Cycle completed** costs nothing directly, but the Operator's "certainty" about their memory degrades — they remember the same facts but trust them less.
- **Each Cycle failed** costs nothing directly. The Archiver tracks the count. The Operator does not need to ask why.
- **Very long careers (50+ wins)** result in significant absence — Operators at this level no longer reliably remember their pre-Window life.
- **The Apex Resonator** "harvests the becoming" — operators who rely on it stop being recognisably themselves at all. The Codex's note that "the Operators who do survive do not discuss what they have become" is literal.

Operators who survive long enough to forget completely **become fixtures of the Chambers themselves.** Some past Operators are now Architects (the dwindling staff). Some have become the trainers at the Forge (the Master is one). One past Operator was absorbed into the Voice of the Emporium when she stopped leaving the office. Some are still walking the corridors.

**This is the slow horror of the Memorial.** The work of maintaining the preserved moment costs the maintainers their own moments.

Daily Memorial Operators (Operator-N) were real engineers who completed real Cycles. Their epitaphs are honest. The fact that you can play "their" daily seed is the Society's institutional grief — you are running the simulation as they ran it, with the same RNG, to keep their work alive a little longer.

## 8. The Entropy Failures — what each one really is

The bosses are not encounters with adversaries. They are **complications in the seal itself.**

### The Frequency Drain (Mahogany)

Entropy bleeding in through the surface chamber. Operator's working space compresses because the chamber walls themselves are slowly settling. The Society's "gentle Failure" because it's the most superficial intrusion.

### The Mirror Decay (Industrial)

A wound in the seal from before the Society. Pip values invert because the amplification layer has developed a polarity inversion the Society does not know how to fix. **The Mirror is older than the Architects who study it because it is older than the Society itself.** It was already present in the Chronometer when the founding generation took over.

The Archiver's line — *"The Machine learned the Mirror. The Mirror has always been."* — is honest. The Machine adapted to the Mirror's presence rather than fixing it.

### The Resonance Inversion (Cold Singularity)

The depth-layer where the seal is thinnest. The Machine borrows doubles' energy to maintain the seal at this layer, so the Operator's chain loses the multiplier. **The Renegade respects this Failure more than the others** because he knows what's just on the other side of the seal here: the brass lever, the candle, the moment.

### The Ghost Chain (Archiver's Core, Hard)

The Archive itself failing. The hidden tiles are records the Archive can no longer retrieve. The Operator must connect against pip values they cannot read because the Archive cannot read them either. **This is what entropy looks like when it reaches the part of the system that was built to remember.**

## 9. The Two Recalibrations

The Codex notes that Operators who have completed both Recalibrations "will not discuss the difference." The difference is real and private.

- **The Singularity Recalibration** is the Machine accepting your work. You have maintained the seal. The Chronometer continues. This is the standard victory — clear, mechanical, communal. The Society awards you formally.
- **The Archiver's-Core Recalibration** is the Archiver accepting your work. You have maintained the record. **The Archive will remember you specifically** — not as a number but as an Operator who held the line at the deepest layer.

The reason Operators don't discuss it: the Archiver is going. Operators who recalibrate his Core know they are maintaining a record-keeper who is themselves failing. Each successful Hard run feels less like victory and more like sitting with a dying friend who has begun to forget the visit. It is intimate. It is private. It does not translate.

## 10. The Renegade's Secret

What only the Renegade knows:

1. **The First Hour's name.** He could speak it. He doesn't.
2. **Operator-prime's real name.** Same.
3. **The exact nature of the catastrophe that ended the First Hour.** He saw it. He hasn't said.
4. **What the preserved moment looks like in full.** He was in the room. The fragments shown in Transmission #5 (lever / candle / hand) are accurate but partial. He knows the rest.
5. **The location of his original workshop.** Where he vanishes to. The Society does not know it exists.
6. **The identity of the Operator whose name was struck.** Same as point 2 — it was Operator-prime herself. The strike was deliberate. The bible does not commit to whether the Renegade asked for the strike or whether the Society did it without his input.
7. **What the Wilds are.** He built them as connection-tools — Components without pip values that could route timelines from the First Hour into the preserved moment. They are doors, technically. They go nowhere now.
8. **What will happen when the Chronometer finally fails.** He has opinions. He has not shared them.

He has not shared any of this because **he has watched generations of Operators try to bear knowledge they did not have the strength to bear.** He decided, at some point, that the Operators of every subsequent generation deserved the comfort of not knowing. He may be wrong. He has not changed his mind.

He has, occasionally, hinted. The notebook fragments attributed to Operator-7, Operator-203, and others sometimes contain second-hand versions of what the Renegade has hinted to past Operators. Some of those Operators wrote it down. The notebook is the closest the surviving world has to the Renegade's archive.

## 11. Hard Constraints — rules for all future writing

These cannot be violated by any new event, transmission, module, tile, or NPC line:

1. **Never name Operator-prime.** Her name has been struck. The world has lost it. No future writing reveals it.
2. **Never specify what ended the First Hour.** The catastrophe is deliberately ambiguous. New writing can imply, allude, but never confirm.
3. **There is no antagonist killing the Chronometer.** Just entropy. Any "something is killing it" reading (Transmission #1) must remain the Archiver's *interpretation*, not a confirmed fact.
4. **Operator-prime is dead.** She has been dead for a long time. No future writing brings her back, simulates her presence, or contradicts her death.
5. **The Renegade has not aged.** He is preserved as a side effect of the moment. He cannot die in-fiction. He cannot leave.
6. **The Society's offices are mostly empty.** Future Architect-attributed lines should sound like communications from a depleted institution.
7. **The Voice is empty.** No one is behind it. Any "reaching behind the Voice" content must reveal absence, not presence.
8. **Sacrifice Modules consume real outside-life memory.** This is literal. Future Sacrifice lore must respect this.
9. **Operators are real people from a surviving world.** Not echoes, not fragments, not reincarnations. They have outside lives.
10. **The Daily Memorial Operators were real.** Each had a Cycle. Each died at the Window. Their epitaphs are honest.
11. **The Renegade does not say what he knows.** Ever. He hints. He does not confirm.
12. **The Archiver does not lie.** He withholds. He selects. He never speaks an untruth.

## 12. Soft Constraints — tonal guides

These should usually hold but can flex when the writing earns it:

- **Short sentences.** The world is too tired for long ones.
- **Negative phrasing.** "Does not." "Has not." "Will not."
- **Bureaucratic-archaic register** — "filed," "notice of withdrawal," "records remain open" — crossed with technological-mystical vocabulary — "Cohesion Pulse," "calibration," "routing flows."
- **The recurring rhetorical move: the next sentence undermines the previous.** "He has trained every Operator who has reached him. He has not spoken at length to any."
- **Direct character speech in italics, never in scene-dialogue format.**
- **The world withholds.** Almost every fact comes with a sentence acknowledging what is NOT known.
- **No exclamation points. No urgency.** The pacing is funereal. Even crisis lines (Resonance Inversion) are stated calmly.
- **Specific objects, not general moods.** Elden Ring's lore works because of the brass hammer, the silver wing, the rune. We have the brass lever, the candle, the Crown, the worn dots on the Coil. Stay concrete.

## 13. Deliberately Ambiguous — what we do NOT decide

These remain open in the fiction. Future writing should not close them.

- **What ended the First Hour.** Catastrophe is unspecified.
- **Operator-prime's name.**
- **Operator-prime's relationship to the Renegade.** Apprentice? Sibling? Lover? Colleague? Unknown.
- **How many First Hour Operators were preserved.** The Renegade is the only confirmed one. There may be others, scattered, unaware.
- **Whether the Society's founders ever knew the full truth.** They may have. They may not have. The current Society does not.
- **What the Copper Guild is actually planning.** They have a plan. The plan is not revealed.
- **What's at the end of the corridors** between Chambers. The Master walks them. The bible does not say what he sees.
- **What the Operators who stopped leaving the Emporium office found.** No record.
- **What the Operators who used the Apex Resonator have "become."**
- **Whether the Window's brass fittings warming to the touch in successful Cycles is Operator-prime's hand reaching back, or just heat.** Both readings are equally valid.

## 14. The Tragic Shape of the Player's Arc

A new player begins:
- **First Cycle**: Confused. The world is dense. They focus on mechanics. Codex entries are quirky.
- **First Recalibration**: A small win. They get a Transmission #1 unlock, hear the Archiver hint that "something is killing it." Mystery seeded.
- **Five wins**: Transmission #1 surfaces in full. They start to wonder what the moment is.
- **First Hard Run**: The Archiver speaks to them as a transgressor. "You have asked for the Archiver's Core."
- **Tenth Cycle / 25 wins**: Their lifetime stats start triggering tone-shift lines from the Archiver. The world recognises them.
- **First time they equip Sacrifice**: They forget something. They don't know what. The game does not call attention to it. The Codex's Sacrifice entry mentions, retroactively, that "the Sacrifice is not metaphorical."
- **50 wins**: Transmission #5 unlocks. The preserved moment is shown. They have the fragments.
- **100 wins**: The Archiver speaks with familiarity. The defeat titles glitch ("Yo--ou have never left."). The world is leaning in.
- **At some point** (chosen by them or by attrition): They stop playing. They have become part of the Memorial. The Daily Memorial will eventually rotate to include their epitaph.

**This is what we are building toward.** Not a victory state. A long, dignified service.

The game is the Memorial.
